Welcome!

I am a Postdoctoral Research Scholar at the School of Politics and Global Studies at Arizona State University. My research investigates political violence, conflict processes, and peace with a focus on three main areas: 1) the strategic decision-making of non-state armed groups, 2) the causes and impacts of local agreements in civil wars, and 3) the international dimensions of civil wars, including the role of organizations, sanctions, and the political economy of conflict. My projects employ a combination of machine learning, forecasting, network analysis, and text-as-data approaches.

My book project, Local Peace Agreements in Civil Wars: Legitimacy, Violence, and Conflict Trajectories, examines why non-state armed groups and communities negotiate and sign local agreements during ongoing wars and how these settlements shape intrastate conflict dynamics. Drawing on an original network dataset of local peace agreements in the Central African Republic, I demonstrate how these agreements recalibrate power balances, influence patterns of civilian victimization, and reshape interactions among armed actors. The first working paper from this project is currently under review at the Journal of Peace Research.

Alongside my book, I pursue related research on international organizations, sanctions, and political economy. In collaboration with Timothy Peterson and Dursun Peksen, I contribute to a new dataset on economic and diplomatic restrictions, generating time-varying indicators of their scope and severity using natural language processing and item response theory.

I received my Ph.D. in Political Science from the University of Pittsburgh. Previously, I completed an M.A. in International Relations at Koç University and a B.A. in Political Science and International Relations at Boğaziçi University.
